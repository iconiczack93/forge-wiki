    PRISM Candidate Scoring System

    PRISM is FORGE's technical candidate evaluator, a port of the glassattempt Chrome extension logic into the Discord bot. It scores candidates against a job description (JD) on a 1-5 scale (0.5 increments) using a three-part framework: technical fit (60%), environment fit (25%), and role-specific signals (15%).

    Overview

    PRISM evaluates candidates by answering three core questions: 1. Can they do the job on day 1-30? (Technical Fit — 60%) 2. Will they thrive here? (Environment Fit — 25%) 3. Do they meet JD-specific requirements? (Role Signals — 15%)

    The scoring is depth-driven, not keyword-driven. A candidate who lists "AI Agents" because they built one RAG chatbot is NOT an agent engineer. Real experience is measured by: - What they actually built (not what they list) - At what companies (tech company vs consulting vs academic) - At what scale (production shipping vs course project)

    The system enforces a critical distinction between hard-to-learn skills (domain expertise, system design patterns) and easy-to-learn skills (specific languages, frameworks). A strong backend engineer missing a specific language is far more valuable than someone who knows the language but has never built the type of system the JD describes.

    Scoring Framework

    1-5 Scale with 0.5 Increments

    Score Definition
    5 Technical bullseye + environment fit — top candidate in any pipeline
    4.5 Strong technical match + clear environment fit — worth fast-tracking
    4 Good technical match, may need ramp on 1-2 easy-to-learn areas — solid interview candidate
    3.5 Partial technical match OR strong adjacent experience that transfers — worth a screen
    3 Relevant background but missing key requirements from the JD
    2.5 Tangentially related — would need significant ramp
    2 General engineer, minimal overlap with what the JD asks for
    1.5 Wrong domain entirely or too junior to be useful
    1 No relevance to the role

    Weighting Breakdown

    Technical Fit (60%) — hands-on experience with JD technologies, production shipping history, real depth vs surface-level keywords

    Environment Fit (25%) — startup/early-stage experience, pace signals (shipped fast, wore many hats, 0-to-1 building), trajectory indicators

    Role Signals (15%) — location, seniority, domain expertise, any explicit JD requirements

    Key Scoring Rules

    1. Score based on what the JD asks for, not what seems impressive
    2. Degree field does not matter — judge by what they have BUILT, not what they studied
    3. Startup experience is a tiebreaker, not a score booster. Two equal candidates? The startup person wins. But startup DNA alone doesn't compensate for missing core tech
    4. Big-company-only experience is a soft negative for early-stage roles, not a hard reject
    5. Past founder/CTO/CEO at small startups are POSITIVE signals if they returned to IC (Individual Contributor) work. Evaluate what they did and what they're doing NOW
    6. Internships don't count as real experience — only full-time roles. Interns-only profiles cap at 2, max 2.5 if exceptional
    7. Degree field irrelevant. Math, physics, self-taught — judge by what they built
    8. Years of experience: use the JD's seniority signal. Don't penalize senior people unless the JD explicitly targets junior candidates

    PRISM V2 System Prompt

    The V2 prompt is a unified instruction set injected into Claude's system context when evaluating candidates. It defines the scoring framework, rules, and critical depth-vs-keywords distinction.

    Key Sections

    DEPTH vs KEYWORDS (CRITICAL)

    This is the most important section. It explicitly warns against keyword-matching:

    Having a keyword on your profile is NOT the same as having real experience. Someone who lists 'AI Agents' because they built one RAG chatbot is NOT an agent engineer.

    Look at WHAT they actually built, at WHAT companies, at WHAT scale. A 'Solutions Engineer' or 'AI Solutions' title at a data labeling company is NOT software engineering.

    5 means you would bet money this person can walk in and BUILD the systems the JD describes. Not that they have touched similar tools. BUILT, SHIPPED, IN PRODUCTION, AT SCALE.

    Hard-to-Learn vs Easy-to-Learn Skills

    The prompt emphasizes that specific languages and frameworks are easy to learn, but domain expertise and system design patterns are hard:

    Distinguish between HARD-TO-LEARN skills (domain expertise, system design patterns) and EASY-TO-LEARN skills (specific languages, frameworks). A strong backend engineer missing a specific language is far more valuable than someone who knows the language but has never built the type of system the JD describes.

    Company Context Signals

    Non-tech companies and roles carry lower weight:

    Non-profit, government, academic, and consulting roles are weaker signals than shipping production software at a tech company. Weight accordingly.

    Leadership Detection

    PRISM includes a local, regex-based leadership detection engine that identifies leadership signals without API calls. This runs before the main evaluation for roles marked as "leadership" in the JD.

    Detected Signals

    The leadership engine uses pattern matching to find:

    • Team Management (led a team of X, managed X engineers)
    • Mentorship (mentored X engineers, mentored junior developers)
    • Hiring (hired, recruited, onboarded engineers)
    • Team Growth (grew team from X to Y)
    • Process/Standards (established engineering practices, defined technical direction)
    • 0→1 Building (built from scratch, greenfield)
    • Founding Engineer (founding engineer, first engineer, employee #X)
    • Early Employee (early engineer, early employee)
    • Cross-Functional Work (cross-functional collaboration, worked with product/design)
    • Architecture Ownership (designed, architected, owned system design)
    • Title Progression (Junior → Engineer → Senior → Staff → Lead/Manager)

    Each signal carries a weighted point value. For example: - Founding engineer: +25 points - Managed X engineers: +15 points - Early engineer: +20 points - Title progression 3+ levels: +20 points

    Maximum leadership score: 100 points.

    Leadership Report

    For leadership roles, PRISM also generates: - Leadership Score (out of 100) - Startup Fit Score (out of 100) - Tech Stack Match (from JD technologies) - Recommendation (Strong/Moderate/Weak/Not Recommended fit)

    Example recommendation thresholds: - Leadership ≥70 + Startup ≥50: Strong Fit - Leadership ≥50 + Startup ≥30: Moderate Fit (verify in interview) - Leadership ≥30: Weak Fit (better for IC role) - Leadership <30: Not Recommended for leadership role

    Tech Stack Matching

    PRISM dynamically extracts technologies from the JD and matches them against the candidate's profile. This is not hardcoded — the tech list is built from the JD content using regex patterns with word boundaries.

    Supported Technologies

    Languages: Node.js, TypeScript, JavaScript, Python, Go, Rust, Java, C++, Ruby, Scala, Kotlin

    Frontend: React, Vue.js, Angular, Next.js

    Backend: FastAPI, Flask, Django, Express

    Data: ClickHouse, PostgreSQL, MySQL, MongoDB, Redis, Elasticsearch, Kafka, DynamoDB

    Cloud: AWS, GCP, Azure

    Infrastructure: Docker, Kubernetes, Terraform

    AI/ML: LangChain, LLM, OpenAI, Anthropic, Braintrust, Cerebras, PyTorch, TensorFlow, ChromaDB, Pinecone

    Matching Algorithm

    1. Extract all tech keywords from JD using regex patterns (with word boundaries to avoid false matches)
    2. Search candidate profile for each technology
    3. Calculate match percentage: found / total
    4. Assign match level:
    5. Strong ≥75% of JD technologies found
    6. Moderate 40-74% found
    7. Weak <40% found

    Example

    JD mentions: Node.js, TypeScript, PostgreSQL, React, Docker

    Candidate has: Node.js, TypeScript, Docker

    Result: Moderate Match (3/5 = 60%)

    FastEval vs V2 Batch

    PRISM supports two evaluation modes, optimized for different workflows.

    FastEval (Single Candidate)

    Used for individual candidate evaluation. Generates a detailed report with:

    Score: [1-5 with 0.5 increments] / 5

Justification:
[One short paragraph explaining overall fit]

Key Points:
- Bullet 1
- Bullet 2
- Bullet 3
- Bullet 4

Why not a 5:
[One or two sentences explaining what held them back]

Sponsorship Check:
Sponsorship Confidence Score: XX%
[Brief explanation of visa sponsorship likelihood]

    Temperature: 0 (deterministic) Max tokens: 2048

    V2 Batch (Multiple Candidates)

    Used for bulk evaluation of 10+ candidates. Outputs JSON only, one object per candidate:

    [
  { "i": 0, "s": 4.5, "r": "strong technical match", "p": "personalization" },
  { "i": 1, "s": 3.0, "r": "relevant but missing key reqs" },
  { "i": 2, "s": 2.0, "r": "minimal overlap" }
]

    Fields: - i — candidate index (from input order) - s — score (1-5 with 0.5 increments) - r — reason (max 15 words) - p — personalization (1-2 sentences as Arda, CTO) — only for 4+ scores

    Automatic Filter: Candidates at their current company <6 months score 1.0 (not reachable).

    Personalization Format (for 4+ scores only): - Direct and specific to their profile - Reference GitHub repos if available, else company/technical work - 1-2 sentences - No greetings or sign-offs - Written as if from Arda (the CTO voice)

    Example: "Your ClickHouse work at Databricks + TypeScript depth is exactly what we need. That scale of data pipeline experience is rare."

    Outreach Integration

    For candidates scoring 3.5 or higher, FORGE automatically generates personalized outreach messages. The system uses a two-stage approach:

    Stage 1: Category Classification

    The [[outreach_personalizer|outreach_personalizer]] module classifies candidates into one of 8 personalization categories using rule-based decision trees:

    1. Active GitHub Builder — 10+ public repos, starred repos
    2. Open Source Contributor — forked repos, contributed
    3. Strong Company Pedigree — top-tier company (Google, Meta, Stripe, etc.)
    4. Direct Skill Match — matches JD technologies
    5. YC/Startup Network — Y Combinator or venture-backed company
    6. Elite Education — Stanford, MIT, CMU, etc.
    7. Working on Interesting Product — notable technical work at current company
    8. Recently Changed Roles — role change within 6 months
    9. Default — strong background, generic

    Stage 2: Template Filling

    Human-written templates for each category are filled with candidate-specific details using Claude Haiku. Output is a 1-2 sentence personalization injected as an Ashby sequence variable.

    Outreach Quality Gate

    Before sending ANY outreach message, the system performs 25-rule validation:

    Hard Bans (fix before sending): - NO em dashes (—) anywhere — use commas, periods, or parentheses - NO "Worth a chat/conversation/call" - NO "on your radar" - NO "I came across your profile" / "I was impressed by" / "caught my eye" - NO filler sentences ("That's a pretty unique combo", "That's exactly the kind of...") - NO time commitments ("15 min", "quick call", "30 min") - NO restating resume back at them

    Format Rules: - 1-2 sentences MAX - Blurb only — no "Hi [Name]" wrapper (Ashby template handles that) - No CTA — personalization is the opener - Close with "Happy to share more if you're curious" or "Open to chatting if you are" - OR ask a genuine question instead of CTA

    Example (Good)

    Saw that you led the infrastructure team at Stripe building their rate-limiting system — that's the exact scale of problem we're solving at HockeyStack. If you're ever curious about shaping GTM infrastructure from scratch, happy to chat.

    Example (Bad)

    I came across your profile and was really impressed by your work at Stripe. We're building something similar and I think you'd be a great fit. Would love to grab a quick 15-min call to chat more about it.
